## Interaction of Radiation with Matter

The interaction of radiation with matter is a fundamental concept in nuclear physics and radiation detection. When energetic particles or photons pass through matter, they transfer energy to atoms of the medium through various physical processes. These interactions form the basis of radiation detection and spectroscopy.

---

## Charged Particle Interactions

Charged particles such as electrons, protons, and alpha particles interact strongly with matter through Coulomb forces. As they pass through a medium, they lose energy primarily by exciting and ionizing atoms along their path.

Heavy charged particles such as alpha particles produce dense ionization tracks and typically have a well-defined range in matter.

Electrons behave differently because of their small mass. They undergo significant scattering and may lose energy through both ionization and bremsstrahlung radiation.

---

## Gamma Ray Interactions

Gamma rays are electrically neutral and interact less frequently with matter. Their interaction occurs through discrete events that transfer energy to electrons in the absorbing material.

The three dominant interaction mechanisms are:

### Photoelectric Effect

In the photoelectric effect, a gamma photon transfers all of its energy to a bound atomic electron, which is then ejected from the atom.

The kinetic energy of the emitted electron is

Eₑ = Eγ − E_b

where Eγ is the photon energy and E_b is the binding energy of the electron.

This interaction dominates at low photon energies and in high atomic number materials.

---

### Compton Scattering

Compton scattering occurs when a gamma photon interacts with a loosely bound or free electron. The photon transfers part of its energy to the electron and is scattered with reduced energy.

This interaction dominates at intermediate gamma-ray energies and produces the Compton continuum observed in gamma-ray spectra.

---

### Pair Production

When the photon energy exceeds 1.022 MeV, a gamma ray can convert into an electron–positron pair in the electric field of a nucleus.

The positron eventually annihilates with an electron producing two photons of 511 keV each. These photons may appear as annihilation peaks in detector spectra.

---

## Importance in Radiation Detection

These interaction processes determine the response of radiation detectors. In high-purity germanium detectors used in gamma-ray spectroscopy, the energy deposited through these mechanisms is converted into electrical signals that allow precise measurement of photon energies.

Understanding these interactions is essential for interpreting experimental spectra and identifying features such as photopeaks, Compton edges, escape peaks, and annihilation peaks.
